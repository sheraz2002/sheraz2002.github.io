6-DOF Serial Manipulator — From CAD to Working Robot

SolidWorks · CoppeliaSim/V-REP · STM32 (Bluepill) · CAN bus · IBT-2 Drivers · Encoders · MATLAB Kinematics

A complete, end-to-end build of a six-degree-of-freedom serial manipulator: CAD modeling, DH-based kinematics (MATLAB), master–slave embedded control over CAN bus, real-time motor control with IBT-2 drivers + encoders, simulation in CoppeliaSim, and a working pick-and-place demo presented at the PIEAS Open House. Key architecture, kinematics, and hardware blocks mirror the design documented in our BS thesis (June 2024).

Specs at a glance: 6 DoF Master + 6 Slaves CAN bus Encoders (500 PPR) PID + Trapezoidal/Cubic Profiles CoppeliaSim validation

1) CAD Model (SolidWorks)

The robot was modeled in SolidWorks with realistic joint limits and mass/inertia approximations. This phase defined link lengths, joint clearances, and the mounting for the gripper and linear base, enabling early collision checking and workspace visualization.

2) Kinematic Modeling (DH → FK/IK)

We derived Denavit–Hartenberg parameters for the link chain and implemented Forward (FK) and Inverse (IK) kinematics in MATLAB. FK was validated by matching the end-effector pose in simulation; IK solutions were checked by re-forwarding angles back to the target pose.

3) Embedded Control Architecture

The system uses a Master–Slave topology over CAN bus: one STM32F103C8T6 master computes IK, converts joint angles to encoder counts, and publishes commands; six STM32F103C8T6 slaves (one per joint) close the local loop using encoder feedback and PID with motion profiles (trapezoidal velocity, cubic polynomial time-slicing).

4) Hardware Build

Electronics were integrated on modular boards: IBT-2 (BTS7960) H-bridge drivers, MCP2551 CAN transceivers, optical/mechanical limit switches, and isolated supplies. Each slave reads its encoder and executes PID at a fixed control period; the master runs IK and trajectory generation.

5) Simulation in CoppeliaSim/V-REP

We validated FK/IK numerically and then reproduced the arm in CoppeliaSim: given a pose from MATLAB FK, the simulated tooltip reached the exact coordinates; given angles from the simulator, MATLAB IK returned the same joint set. We also ran workspace sweeps and collision checks before hardware motion.

6) Motion Profiles & Motor Control

  • Trapezoidal velocity for smooth accel/constant/decel without overshoot; jerk minimized versus step commands.
  • Cubic polynomial + time slicing to respect joint limits and synchronize arrivals across axes.
  • PID on each slave, tuned per joint inertia/friction; encoders (500 PPR) ensure precise counts per revolution.
  • Limit switches (optical U-shaped, mechanical on prismatic axis) guard motion bounds and enable homing.

7) Practical Experiment — Pick & Place

For the bench demo, the master generated a synchronized trajectory: home → approach → grasp → lift → place → retreat. Slaves executed the profile with PID and encoder feedback; limit switches enforced safe envelopes. We verified repeatability over multiple cycles, then increased speed while maintaining stability and current limits on the IBT-2 bridges. (Validated first in CoppeliaSim, then on hardware.)

8) Open House @ PIEAS

The working prototype and simulation results were exhibited at the PIEAS Open House, showcasing end-to-end engineering: CAD → kinematics → control → simulation → hardware. Visitors could observe trajectory planning on the laptop (MATLAB/CoppeliaSim) and watch the robot perform pick-and-place on the table rig.

What’s in the Code Repo?

  • Master (STM32F103): IK solver, trajectory generator, CAN publisher.
  • Slaves ×6 (STM32F103): PID position loops, encoder capture (EXTI/timers), PWM to IBT-2, homing.
  • Utilities: DH parameter file, MATLAB FK/IK scripts, CoppeliaSim scene for validation.

Build toolchain: STM32CubeMX → Keil µVision; simulation in CoppeliaSim; kinematics notebooks in MATLAB.
